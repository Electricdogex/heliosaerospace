The Phoenix

From orbital hotel to interplanetary spacecraft to space station

Origin

Phoenix began life as the Polaris orbital hotel. After the Polaris accident, Helios purchased the ruined station in 1992 and repurposed it as the backbone of a spinning Mars ship. The craft was constructed between 1992 and 1994, incorporating Helios’ in‑house Osprey‑methane engines and a centrifugal gravity ring. Dev Ayesa originally planned to automate the ship, but Ed Baldwin insisted on a manual configuration.

Mission & Crew

Phoenix launched in 1994 as Helios’ crewed vehicle to Martian orbit. The ship’s spacious habitation ring — a remnant of its hotel heritage — allowed the crew to travel comfortably and supported research modules and a hydroponic garden. Attached to the front of the ring were multiple landing pods carrying HAB rovers, drills and fuel production equipment for the Mars base. A dedicated Mars Surface Access Module (MSAM) nicknamed “Popeye” ferried personnel and cargo between Phoenix and the Martian surface.

Transformation into a Station

After the 1994 mission, Phoenix remained in Mars orbit. In the late 1990s the ship transitioned into a permanent space and transit station. By the early 2000s Phoenix served as a hub for Helios’ Unity transport vehicle, facilitating crew and cargo transfers for the Mars‑7 Alliance.
